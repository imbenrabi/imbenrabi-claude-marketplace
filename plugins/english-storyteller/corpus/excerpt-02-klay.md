# Corpus Excerpt 02 — Phil Klay, *Redeployment* (2014)

**Genre:** Iraq/Afghanistan war literary fiction, short story collection
**Relevance:** contemporary military realism, terse military register, veteran psychology, jargon-as-authenticity, irony as dissociation
**Use for:** military jargon without explanation, terse register under emotional load, returning veteran psychology, gap between military and civilian

---

## Excerpted Passage (from "Redeployment")

> We shot dogs. Not by accident. We did it on purpose, and we called it "Operation Scooby." I'm a dog person, so I thought about it a lot.
>
> First time was instinct. I hear a shot, I hit the deck, roll to cover — I'm thinking, "We're taking fire!" But it's just Sergeant Garza executing a dog. The dog's yelping, and Garza's saying, "Shut up, shut up," and then it stops.
>
> After that it was a platoon thing. Dogs were everywhere in Fallujah. They were a problem. They'd eat the garbage that piled up when we were in firefights, and sometimes they'd eat the bodies. The higher-ups decided they had to go, so you'd be on post and some dog would come up looking for food and you'd have to shoot it. I didn't enjoy it.

## Technique Annotations

### Technique 1: Institutional Abbreviation as Emotional Compression
"Operation Scooby." The military gives missions names that normalize them — and Klay lets this name land without commentary. The gap between the pop-culture reference (Scooby-Doo) and the activity (killing dogs) is the entire emotional content of the paragraph. The narrator doesn't need to note the irony because the reader cannot miss it.

**Craft move to extract:** When military/institutional language normalizes horror, present the institutional term unadorned, without authorial comment. The reader will supply the horror. Authorial comment would dilute it.

### Technique 2: "I'm a dog person"
This single sentence of character information changes the entire weight of the passage. Without it, the story is about military procedure. With it, the story is about what procedure does to a person. The information is delivered flatly, in the same register as everything else.

**Craft move to extract:** Deliver the detail that recontextualizes everything else in the same register and sentence structure as surrounding narration. Do not flag it as important. Let the reader feel the weight alone.

### Technique 3: Terse Register Under Emotional Load
"I didn't enjoy it." Not "I was disturbed by it," not "it haunted me," not "I knew something had changed in me." The sentence is simultaneously honest and blocked — it performs the emotional withholding it describes. The flat sentence IS the psychological content.

**Craft move to extract:** The character reports internal states in the flattest possible language. "I didn't enjoy it" is not understatement — it is the maximum statement the narrator's psychology permits. The gap between the act and the language is the gap in the narrator's psyche.

### Technique 4: Jargon Without Translation
"hit the deck, roll to cover" — no explanation. "Sergeant Garza executing a dog" — "executing" is military/clinical vocabulary for a dog killing. The narrator uses military jargon because they are military. The reader must keep up. The refusal to translate for civilians is part of the narrator's voice.

**Craft move to extract:** Never translate military jargon for the civilian reader. The words are correct for who is speaking them. The civilian gap is the reader's problem, which is exactly what returning veterans experience.

### Technique 5: Paragraph as Sequence, Not Scene
Klay's paragraphs do not build to a scene — they build to a fact. Each paragraph advances the logic of how something became normal. "First time was instinct... After that it was a platoon thing." This is the logic of institutional normalization, rendered in paragraph structure.

**Craft move to extract:** When narrating how something became normal (dissociation, habituation to violence), structure the prose as a sequence of normalizations. Each paragraph = the next step in the logic.

## Application to Military Realism / Dark Psychology

The source chapter's narrator watches himself act, references himself in third person ("זה שמביט"), and notes emotional states in the flattest possible terms. Klay's register — terse, jargon-dense, flat affect as the maximum permitted emotion — is the closest English analogue to this narrator's dissociation structure.
