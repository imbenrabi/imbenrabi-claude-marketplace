# Carver — Iceberg Theory

## Technique Annotations

### `iceberg_theory`

What is actually happening in a Carver story is almost never stated. The surface content
— a man and his wife hosting her blind friend for dinner — is not the story. The story is
the narrator's failure of empathy and the moment he transcends it. But this is never said.
The reader infers from the accumulated evidence of the narrator's flat, defensive voice.

The iceberg: 7/8 of the story is below water. The 1/8 visible is the surface action.
The reader dives for the rest.

**How to apply:** decide what the story is actually about (the submerged content). Then
write only the surface. Trust the reader to reconstruct the depth from the angle of the
light on the surface.

### `declarative_suppression`

Carver's prose is flat. It does not rise to meet its emotions. The most important moments
are described in the same register as the least important. The dinner is described the
same way the narrator's shame is described. The suppression is the damage.

**Illustrative example (in Carver's style):**

> My wife went to sleep early. I stayed up. I drank a little and watched the TV.
> The blind man sat next to me. I didn't know what to say to him.
> "What's on?" he said.
> I told him.
> "Is that all?" he said.
> I said that was all there was tonight.

**What this demonstrates:**
- Prose density: nothing can be cut without losing content
- Show don't tell: the narrator's inadequacy is in the dialogue, not stated
- Character voice: the flatness is the voice; the voice is the character

### `dialogue_as_revelation`

In Carver, dialogue reveals character not through what characters say but through what
they cannot say. The narrator does not know how to be present with another person. His
dialogue shows this — his answers are minimal, evasive, incomplete. The dialogue is a
record of failure.

**How to apply:** dialogue is not information exchange. Dialogue is a record of what
characters can and cannot do with each other. The gap between what is asked and what
is answered is the story.

## Rhythm Example

Carver's characteristic rhythm:

```
[Short declarative: 5-7 words]
[Short declarative: 5-7 words]
[Short declarative: 6-8 words]
[Slightly longer sentence that breaks the pattern: 10-12 words]
[Short declarative: 4-5 words]
```

The uniformity is intentional — this is the `literary-minimalism` exception to the
rhythm variety rule. The monotone IS the style. The judge should NOT penalize rhythm
variety for passages anchored to this preset and this author.

## Application Rules for This Plugin

When `literary-minimalism` preset and Carver as anchor:

1. The surface content is not the story; the story is submerged
2. Dialogue records what characters cannot do, not what they say
3. Uniform short sentences are intentional — do NOT vary length the way other presets require
4. Never state the emotion the scene produces; trust the accumulated surface to imply it
