# Corpus Excerpt 01 — Tim O'Brien, *The Things They Carried* (1990)

**Genre:** Vietnam War literary fiction / autofiction
**Relevance:** military realism, catalog-as-emotional-weight, collective/individual POV, guilt structure
**Use for:** list technique, weight of objects as proxy for psychological burden, short declarative puncture

---

## Excerpted Passage

> The things they carried were largely determined by necessity. Among the necessities or near-necessities were P-38 can openers, pocket knives, heat tabs, wristwatches, dog tags, mosquito repellent, chewing gum, candy, cigarettes, salt tablets, packets of Kool-Aid, lighters, matches, sewing kits, Military Payment Certificates, C rations, and two or three canteens of water. Together, these items weighed between 15 and 20 pounds, depending on a man's habits or rate of metabolism. Henry Dobbins, who was a big man, carried extra rations; he was especially fond of canned peaches in heavy syrup over pound cake. Dave Jensen, who practiced field hygiene, carried a toothbrush, dental floss, and several hotel-size bars of soap he'd stolen from a Red Cross trailer. Ted Lavender, who was scared, carried tranquilizers until he was shot in the head outside the village of Than Khe in mid-April.

---

## Technique Annotations

### Technique 1: Catalog as Weight
The list begins with mundane military objects and proceeds without commentary. The weight is literal (pounds) before it is psychological. By the time the list ends, the weight has transferred to the reader. O'Brien never tells you the men are burdened — he shows you the contents of their packs.

**Craft move to extract:** When conveying psychological burden in military realism, catalog the physical objects before naming the burden. The list IS the burden. Do not follow the list with a summary statement about what it means.

### Technique 2: Short Declarative Puncture
"Ted Lavender, who was scared, carried tranquilizers until he was shot in the head outside the village of Than Khe in mid-April."

The sentence is still in list mode — parallel structure to "Henry Dobbins, who was a big man" — but it ends with a death. The reader is not prepared because the grammar didn't prepare them. The short declarative that should close the catalog (death) arrives with the same syntactic weight as "dental floss."

**Craft move to extract:** Introduce violent fact inside list syntax, without changing register. The refusal to shift register is the power. The grammar says this death is the same weight as the dental floss.

### Technique 3: Parenthetical Character as Destiny
"who was scared" — the character attribute is a parenthetical. But it is also his fate. He was scared, and then he was dead. The parenthetical contains the entire arc.

**Craft move to extract:** Character defining attributes can be embedded as parentheticals within action sentences. The parenthetical then resonates differently when the action ends badly.

### Technique 4: Specificity Over Abstraction
"canned peaches in heavy syrup over pound cake" — not "extra food" or "dessert items." The specific brand of comfort humanizes Dobbins before the narrative needs that humanity. O'Brien never uses generic nouns when specific ones are available.

**Craft move to extract:** Every object is named at the level of specificity a soldier would know it. No generic nouns.

---

## Application to Military Realism / Dark Psychology

In source material depicting October 7 or similar events: when a narrator lists equipment, locations, and personnel in a sequence, let the list bear the weight without emotional annotation. The fact of the list — its length, its specificity, its continuation through horror — is the psychological content.
