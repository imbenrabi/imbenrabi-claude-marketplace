# Corpus Excerpt 05 — Anthony Loyd, *My War Gone By, I Miss It So* (1999)

**Genre:** War memoir / literary nonfiction
**Relevance:** Bosnia war journalism, addiction as metaphor for war's pull, dark psychology of violence attraction, lyrical prose that doesn't flinch, body as recording instrument
**Use for:** lyrical prose under atrocity, dissociation through beauty, the body as witness separate from the self, addiction structure applied to combat psychology

## Representative Passage (reconstructed from knowledge)

> The shelling of Sarajevo was beautiful in the way that things you know will kill you are sometimes beautiful. I tried not to look. I always looked.
>
> Each impact lit the hills for a half-second. In that light you could see the villas and the pine trees, the ski runs, the church spires, and above them the gun positions. In that light Sarajevo looked like a model of itself: too detailed, too small, something a child had made with great care and then decided to destroy.
>
> I took pictures. The pictures were the evidence that something had happened here, and also the evidence that I had been there when it happened. I could not tell anymore which need was stronger.

## Technique Annotations

### Technique 1: Beauty as Complicity
"The shelling of Sarajevo was beautiful in the way that things you know will kill you are sometimes beautiful." Loyd does not flinch from his own aesthetic response to atrocity. The beauty is not prettified — it is admitted, and the admission is the first act of moral honesty. A narrator who refuses to admit finding violence beautiful has less authority than one who admits it and continues.

**Craft move to extract:** When the narrator witnesses atrocity and finds some element of it beautiful or compelling, admit this without shame or deflection. The honest admission of an uncomfortable aesthetic response is more authoritative than performed revulsion.

### Technique 2: The Divided Witness ("I tried not to look. I always looked.")
Two sentences. Opposite propositions. Both true simultaneously. This is not a contradiction — it is the structure of compulsion. The narrator knows the behavior is wrong by their own standards and continues it anyway. The declarative opposition is more powerful than any explanation of the psychology.

**Craft move to extract:** When the narrator's behavior contradicts their stated intention or value, state both as parallel declaratives. Do not explain the contradiction. "I tried not to. I always did." The reader understands.

### Technique 3: Light as Revealer
"In that light you could see the villas and the pine trees, the ski runs, the church spires, and above them the gun positions." The list of peaceful objects — villas, ski runs, church spires — accumulates before the gun positions arrive. The light that destroys also reveals the order being destroyed. The technique: let the peace inventory precede the violence indicator, in the same sentence, as a list.

**Craft move to extract:** When violence reveals what it is destroying, list the civilian/peaceful elements first in catalog form, then append the military/destructive element at the end of the same catalog. The list structure performs the relationship — they are all equal now, all illuminated by the same explosion.

### Technique 4: Scale Collapse
"Sarajevo looked like a model of itself: too detailed, too small, something a child had made with great care and then decided to destroy." The city becomes miniature — not metaphorically smaller, but literally perceived as a scale model. The scale collapse is a dissociation mechanism: when something is too large to process, the mind miniaturizes it. Loyd names this without calling it dissociation.

**Craft move to extract:** When a scene exceeds the narrator's cognitive capacity, let them report it as smaller than it is. Not "it seemed unreal" — something specific and spatial: "it looked like a model," "it was the size of a paragraph," "it had the scale of something from a distance."

### Technique 5: The Camera as Ethical Problem
"I could not tell anymore which need was stronger." The narrator photographs atrocity for documentation and for personal proof of presence. These two needs are indistinguishable. Loyd does not resolve the ethical problem — he names the indistinguishability. The refusal to resolve is the ethical position.

**Craft move to extract:** When the narrator's motives are genuinely mixed (help vs. witness vs. self-preservation vs. compulsion), name all of them and admit the inability to rank them. Do not manufacture a hierarchy the narrator does not actually have.

## Application to Military Realism / Dark Psychology

The source chapter's narrator watches the burning Toyota, the baby stroller, the body parts on the road — and notes them with compressed precision. Loyd's template: admit what the witnessing does to you, name the aesthetic experience if it exists, do not perform emotions that would be more comfortable to have.

The source narrator's note — "filing this for later use" — is Loyd's camera. The dissociated witness who records what they cannot yet process, for a future self who may or may not be able to use it.
