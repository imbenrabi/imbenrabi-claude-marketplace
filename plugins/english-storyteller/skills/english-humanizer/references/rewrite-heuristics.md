# Rewrite Heuristics

Per-pattern transformation strategies with before/after examples.

---

## H1: Filter Words → Direct Scene

**Strategy:** Remove the filter layer. Put the sensation or fact directly into the narration.
The reader is the camera. The POV character is not reporting; they are experiencing.

**Before:**
> "She noticed the glass was empty. She saw him reach for the bottle."

**After:**
> "The glass was empty. He reached for the bottle."

**Before:**
> "He heard the argument from the next room and realized it was getting worse."

**After:**
> "The argument from the next room was getting worse."

**Rule:** If removing "she noticed" / "he heard" / "she realized" and keeping the rest
produces a grammatical sentence — do it. That's the fix.

**Free indirect discourse option:** When the filter is carrying character interiority,
convert to free indirect:
- "She thought that he might leave" → "He might leave."
- "He wondered if she knew" → "Did she know?"

---

## H2: Named Emotions → Enacted Behavior

**Strategy:** Delete the emotion-statement. Find the physical or behavioral equivalent
that lets the reader reconstruct the emotion.

**Before:**
> "She felt grief wash over her. She was devastated by the news."

**After:**
> "She picked up the phone and put it down without calling anyone."

**Before:**
> "He was terrified."

**After:**
> "His hands wouldn't stay still."

**Before:**
> "She felt hope for the first time in months."

**After:**
> "She made a list. Crossed two things off before she'd finished writing them."

**Rule:** The enacted behavior should be specific and slightly unexpected — not the first
physical symptom of the emotion but the second or third, the one that the character
themselves might not recognize as being about the emotion.

---

## H3: Uniform Rhythm → Cadence Variation

**Strategy:** Break the metronome. Introduce a fragment. Follow a long sentence with
a very short one. Move the content of the longest sentence to a structural position
where its length serves a purpose (accumulation, memory, interiority).

**Before:**
> "He walked to the car. He unlocked the door. He got in. He drove away."

**After:**
> "He walked to the car, unlocked it, got in — and sat there for a minute before he
> drove away."

**Or:**
> "He walked to the car. Inside was all the same. He drove."

**Rule:** Identify what the rhythm should feel like at this moment in the story. Panic:
short, staccato, fragments. Waiting: long, accumulating. Aftermath: short, numb. Make
the rhythm serve the emotion.

---

## H4: Adverb Stacking → Precise Verb

**Strategy:** Replace [weak verb + adverb] with [precise verb]. If a precise verb doesn't
exist, use a physical action that implies the quality.

| Before | After |
|---|---|
| "walked slowly" | "moved toward" / "drifted to the window" |
| "spoke quietly" | "said" (if tone is in the content) / "said this low enough that..." |
| "looked carefully" | "examined" / "went over it twice" |
| "ran quickly" | "ran" / "sprinted" / "covered the ground" |
| "said angrily" | physical beat + "said" / or just "said" |
| "smiled warmly" | "smiled" / or omit if redundant |

**For intensifiers:**
- "very angry" → find the behavior
- "extremely sad" → find the object or action
- "incredibly beautiful" → one specific detail that makes the reader agree

## M1: Hedged Qualifiers → Commitment

**Strategy:** Remove the hedge. State the thing. If you're not sure the thing is true,
that uncertainty should be structural, not lexical.

**Before:**
> "The room seemed rather empty and somehow sad."

**After:**
> "The room was empty. A chair on its side. Nothing else."

**Rule:** "Seemed" is allowed when the narrator genuinely cannot know (POV limitation).
"Seemed" as a hedge against overstating = always cut.

---

## M2: Over-Complete Dialogue → Fragmented Dialogue

**Strategy:** Cut the part of the answer that directly responds to the question.
Leave the remainder. Add a physical beat if the silenced part carries emotional weight.

**Before:**
> "Where were you last night?" she asked.
> "I was at home watching television," he answered.

**After:**
> "Where were you."
> He looked at his phone.
> "Home."

**Non-answer pattern:**
**Before:**
> "Do you still love her?" he asked.
> "I don't know," she replied honestly.

**After:**
> "Do you still love her."
> She refilled her glass.

---

## M3: Context-Setting Opening → In Media Res

**Strategy:** Cut the time/weather/establishing sentence. Begin with the first action
or the first piece of charged dialogue.

**Before:**
> "It was late October and the weather had turned cold. Sarah had been sitting at the
> kitchen table for twenty minutes when the phone rang."

**After:**
> "The phone rang. She'd been sitting there long enough for the coffee to go cold."

**Or (if time/weather is essential):**
> Fold it into action: "She drove home in the first real cold of October, the heater
> making a sound she'd been ignoring since August."

---

## M4: Symmetrical Prose → Asymmetric Truth

**Strategy:** Break the parallel. Change the grammar or length of one clause so the two
halves don't mirror each other. The asymmetry is where the specific truth lives.

**Before:**
> "He loved her but feared her. She needed him but resented him."

**After:**
> "He loved her the way he feared her — the same quality, something he couldn't name."

**Or:**
> "He loved her. She needed him. Neither knew what to do with the difference."

---

## General Transform Rules

1. **Delete before rewriting.** If a sentence survives the deletion test, keep it. Otherwise, cut it.
2. **Trust the content.** If the content is doing its work, the reader doesn't need the narrator's
   interpretation.
3. **Asymmetry is naturalness.** Break any pattern that repeats more than twice.
4. **The body knows before the mind explains.** Physical action before interiority.
5. **Dialogue should disappoint.** Remove the part that directly answers the question.
6. **The second thought is the true thought.** The first physical response to emotion is a cliché;
   the second or third is specific.
