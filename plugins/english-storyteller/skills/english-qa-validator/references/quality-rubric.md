# English Quality Rubric

Scoring anchors for each dimension at levels 2, 5, 8, and 10. Use these examples to
calibrate scores. **Pass threshold: all dimensions ≥ 9/10.**

---

## Dimension 1: Rhythm

Does sentence length vary with structural purpose?

**Score 2 — Complete metronome:**
> "He walked to the door. He opened it slowly. He looked outside. He saw nothing."

Four sentences, all 5–7 words, all subject-verb structure, all past-tense declarative.
The reader can tap their foot to it.

**Score 5 — Some variation, but mechanical:**
> "He walked to the door, which had been locked since morning, and turned the handle.
> He opened it. He looked outside. Nothing was there."

One long sentence followed by three short ones — noticeable pattern, but still predictable.

**Score 8 — Real variation, mostly purposeful:**
> "He opened the door. The street was empty — not quiet-empty but actually empty, as if
> something had moved through and cleared it. He stood in the doorway for a moment.
> Then went in."

Short/medium-long/medium/fragment. Variation serves meaning. Minor: "for a moment" is filler.

**Score 10 — Rhythm as meaning:**
> "He opened the door. Nothing. The street ran three blocks to the water and there was
> not one person on it, not one car, not one light in any window — just the street and
> the water and the dark. He went in. Locked it behind him."

Short/fragment/very long (accumulation)/short/short. The long sentence is the absence
rendered enormous. The short endings are the shutting down of feeling.

## Dimension 2: Character Voice

Could you remove the dialogue tags and still know who is speaking?

**Score 2 — All voices identical:**
> "I think the situation has become quite serious," he said.
> "Yes, I agree that it is serious and we should do something about it," she replied.

Both characters speak in the same register, complete sentences, same syntax. Interchangeable.

**Score 5 — Distinguishable but inconsistent:**
> "It's bad," he said.
> "I think we should be concerned about where this is going," she replied.

He is terse; she is more formal. Distinct, but her register drifts toward generic
in the longer speech.

**Score 8 — Distinct and mostly consistent:**
> "It's bad." He didn't look at her.
> "How bad?" she said. "Give me a number."

His terseness is characterized; her need for quantification is a voice trait. Minor:
"Give me a number" is slightly too convenient.

**Score 10 — Voices could not be transposed:**
> "Bad enough." He checked the window again.
> She did the math out loud, fast, not for his benefit.
> "So we have maybe six hours. Before anyone notices."

His voice is pure compression. Her interiority (doing math out loud) is a behavioral
signature. The beat between them is held, not explained.

## Dimension 3: Continuity

Are timeline, objects, and emotional states consistent?

**Score 2 — Explicit contradictions:**
A character left their bag in the car in scene 1; they pull something from it in scene 3
without explanation. Or a character who was established as not knowing something acts on
that knowledge three scenes later.

**Score 5 — Inconsistencies recoverable by context:**
A character's emotional state shifts without transition, but the reader can construct
a reason. Minor object inconsistencies. Timeline slightly fuzzy.

**Score 8 — Consistent with minor ambiguities:**
All objects and locations track correctly. Emotional arc is clear and causally motivated.
One or two moments where the passage of time is ambiguous.

**Score 10 — Airtight, every detail tracks:**
Every named object has a consistent position. Every character knows only what they've
been told or could observe. Emotional arcs follow causality. Time jumps handled explicitly
in the prose.

## Dimension 4: Prose Density

Can every sentence survive the deletion test?

**Score 2 — Heavily padded:**
> "He stood for a moment, thinking about what had just happened, reflecting on the
> events of the previous hour. It was a difficult situation for everyone involved.
> He wasn't sure what to do next."

All three sentences either restate or describe without advancing. All can be deleted.

**Score 5 — Some padding, mostly functional:**
> "He stood for a moment. The hour had been difficult. He wasn't sure what to do."

"The hour had been difficult" is still evaluation rather than fact. Could be cut.

**Score 8 — Mostly lean, one weak sentence:**
> "He stood in the hall. Not sure what to do next."

"Not sure what to do next" is still evaluation. Cut or replace with an action.

**Score 10 — Nothing survives the deletion test:**
> "He stood in the hall. The door was still open."

Both sentences carry information. "The door was still open" implies he hasn't acted,
suggests paralysis, and is a concrete fact. Nothing can come out.

## Dimension 5: POV Consistency

Does the narrative perspective hold?

**Score 2 — Frequent unintentional shifts:**
> "She left without saying goodbye. He watched her go, feeling a mix of relief and sadness.
> On the other side of the city, her sister was also awake, thinking about their argument."

Three POVs in three sentences. Classic AI head-hopping.

**Score 5 — Mostly consistent with one drift:**
> "She left without saying goodbye. He watched her go. She was thinking about the flight."

Third sentence breaks POV — the narrator suddenly has access to her interiority.

**Score 8 — Consistent, one ambiguous moment:**
> "She left without saying goodbye. He watched her go. He wondered if she knew."

Consistently in his POV. "He wondered if she knew" is correctly attributed. Minor
ambiguity: what she knows could be more precise.

**Score 10 — Locked POV, any shift is explicit:**
> "She left without saying goodbye. He let her go. He didn't wonder about it —
> there was nothing to wonder about."

Locked in his POV. "He didn't wonder" is an interior statement fully owned by
the perspective. No leakage.

## Dimension 6: Emotional Register

Does the emotional temperature match the scene's function?

**Score 2 — Tonally incoherent:**
> "He pointed the gun at her head. It was quite an unusual Saturday, she reflected."

Violent scene + ironic distance colliding without craft. The tonal break is accidental.

**Score 5 — Mostly consistent, one accidental break:**
A funeral scene sustains grief in the narration but one sentence accidentally becomes
comic due to word choice. Or an action scene sustains urgency but lingers on a metaphor.

**Score 8 — Consistent register with intentional modulation:**
The register is stable. One moment of deliberate tonal shift that earns its place
(e.g., a dark comic beat in a grief scene that makes the grief worse, not better).

**Score 10 — Register serves every beat:**
The emotional temperature rises and falls in calibrated steps. Every shift is intentional.
The reader feels the register change as structural, not accidental.

## Dimension 7: Show Don't Tell

Are emotions enacted rather than named?

**Score 2 — Predominantly told:**
> "She was devastated. He felt guilty. They were both sad. It was an emotional moment."

Every emotional state named directly. No behavior. No implication.

**Score 5 — Some showing, some telling mixed:**
> "She was devastated. She sat down on the floor and didn't get up."

The action is there but the named emotion precedes it and renders it redundant.

**Score 8 — Mostly shown, minor labels:**
> "She sat down on the floor and didn't get up. It had been a bad day."

"Bad day" is a mild label but its vagueness almost works. The action carries the weight.

**Score 10 — All affect implied:**
> "She sat down on the floor. Not the couch, not a chair — the floor. And she stayed."

No named emotion. "The floor" is specific and unexpected enough to imply collapse.
"And she stayed" completes the implication of inability to move.

## Dimension 8: Reference Fidelity

Does the prose reflect the identified benchmark works?

This dimension is N/A if no `references.json` exists.

**Score 2 — No correspondence to benchmarks:**
The prose was generated without consulting the benchmark works. It reads as generic
literary fiction. The annotated techniques from the anchor work are absent.

**Score 5 — Surface correspondence only:**
The prose applies one annotated technique but superficially — the form is present but
the function is not. (e.g., lists things, but the list doesn't accumulate emotional weight.)

**Score 8 — 1–2 techniques applied correctly:**
Two of the annotated techniques are clearly present and functioning. The prose sounds like
it was written in conversation with the benchmark work. One technique is underdeveloped.

**Score 10 — 2–3 techniques applied precisely:**
Three annotated techniques are clearly present, functioning as the benchmark uses them,
and integrated into the prose naturally (not as imitation). The prose would not surprise
a reader of the anchor work.

## Override Notes

**Do NOT penalize intentional brevity.** The `literary-minimalism` preset intentionally
scores lower on rhythm variety — the monotone is the style. Note preset when scoring.

**Do NOT penalize formal register.** Some narrators and characters are legitimately formal.
Score register *consistency*, not register *level*.

**Reference Fidelity score is N/A** when no `references.json` exists. Exclude from average.

**Pass threshold:** ALL dimensions ≥ 9/10 (not an average).
**FLAG:** any dimension 5–8 — targeted fix pass.
**FAIL:** any dimension < 5 — full humanizer pass.
