# Prose Patterns

Working examples of rhythm transforms, sentence structures, and opening techniques.
Load before generating prose. Use these as the target form, not as templates to copy.

---

## Rhythm Patterns

### Short-Long-Short (tension-accumulation-release)

Use at: scene peaks, moments of waiting, the pause before violence.

```
Short:   He stopped.
Long:    The hallway was the same hallway it had always been — same buckled linoleum,
         same bare bulb at the far end, same smell of something burned a long time ago
         and never aired out.
Short:   He went in anyway.
```

### Accumulation (O'Brien-style list as weight)

Use at: cataloguing what is carried, what is left behind, what is owed.

```
They carried the land itself — the red dust, the gray elephant grass, the paddy water,
the piss and shit of a whole continent, the stench of death, the weight of the dead.
They carried their own lives. The pressures were enormous.
```

### Fragment Sequence (Johnson-style)

Use at: aftermath, shock, dissociation, unreliable perception.

```
The man was down. Or the man had been down. Hard to say. It was bright. Then it wasn't.
Somebody was talking. Somebody had been talking for a while.
```

### Long Breath (Robinson-style)

Use at: meditation, memory, the sacred in the ordinary.

```
There is a kind of light in late afternoon, when the sun is already half behind the
line of hills and the world is lit not from above but from within, from the gold stored
in the grass and the dust, that makes a person wonder whether beauty is not a debt —
something owed, something being paid back for the suffering that was ordinary
and went unremarked.
```

## Scene Entry Patterns

### In-Media-Res (action entry)

**Never:** "It was Tuesday when John arrived at the checkpoint."
**Always:** Entry into a moment already in progress.

Pattern A — Verb first:
> "The gate was open. Nobody explained it."

Pattern B — Dialogue entry:
> "Where's the sergeant?" He asked the kid standing at the wire. The kid didn't know.

Pattern C — Sensation entry:
> "The smell reached him before anything else. Sweet and wrong."

Pattern D — End of motion:
> "He stopped running. He wasn't sure when."

## Filter-Word Replacement Patterns

| Filter version | Direct version |
|---|---|
| "She saw the truck come around the corner" | "The truck came around the corner" |
| "He heard the dog barking somewhere behind the house" | "The dog was barking behind the house" |
| "She noticed his hands were shaking" | "His hands were shaking" |
| "He realized the door had been open the whole time" | "The door had been open the whole time" |
| "She felt something shift between them" | "Something shifted between them" |
| "He watched as she turned and walked away" | "She turned and walked away" |
| "She thought that maybe she should leave" | "Maybe she should leave" (free indirect) |

## Show-Don't-Tell Replacement Patterns

| Told | Shown |
|---|---|
| "She was terrified" | "She counted the money twice. Put it in her pocket. Counted it again." |
| "He felt grief" | "He picked up her mug from the dish rack. Dried it. Didn't put it away." |
| "She was angry" | "She folded the letter carefully before she tore it in half." |
| "He was relieved" | "He sat down on the floor with his back against the wall. Just sat." |
| "She felt connected to him" | "She said nothing. Neither did he. It was fine." |
| "He was ashamed" | "He drove past the house twice before he parked." |

## Dialogue Fragmentation Patterns

Real dialogue is incomplete, deflecting, and interrupted.

**Over-complete:**
> "Why didn't you come home last night?" she asked.
> "I was working late and lost track of the time," he replied.

**Fragmented (realistic):**
> "Where were you."
> He didn't look up. "Working."
> "Until three."
> He didn't answer that.

**Non-answer:**
> "Did you know about it?"
> She looked at the window.
> "The window's dirty," she said.

**Interruption:**
> "I just wanted to say—"
> "No." She stood up. "Not tonight."

## Prose Density Patterns

### The Deletion Audit

After writing, apply the deletion test to each sentence:
1. Remove the sentence.
2. Does the reader lose information they cannot recover?
3. If NO — delete it. If YES — keep it.

Common cut targets:
- "He turned and went to the door." (The next line shows him at the door — cut.)
- "It was a difficult situation for everyone involved." (Tells what scene shows — cut.)
- "She thought about everything that had happened." (Vague summary — cut.)
- "Time passed." (If the next scene shows time has passed — cut.)
- "He didn't know what to do." (If the character's behavior shows this — cut.)
