# English Register Profiles

Eight register archetypes for character voice design. Each character should be assigned
one primary register. Narrator register is separate from character registers.

---

## 1. Terse / Military / Operational

**Who uses it:** soldiers, security personnel, characters under extreme operational stress.

**Characteristics:**
- Verb-first or subject-verb, no subordination
- No emotional language in operational context
- Technical terminology used precisely
- Silence as communication — does not fill space
- Sentence length: very short (3–7 words)
- Fragmentation: high (incomplete sentences are functional)
- No self-explanation; no interiority in operational dialogue

**Example:**
> "Clear." He moved to the next doorway. "Clear." Nobody argued with him.

---

## 2. Educated / Professional

**Who uses it:** intellectuals, officers in non-operational contexts, educated antagonists.

**Characteristics:**
- Complex syntax with subordinate clauses
- Precise word choice; no slang
- Hedged in argument ("it seems", "one might say") but not in command
- Sentence length: medium to long (12–20 words)
- Fragmentation: low

**Example:**
> "The situation is not, as you seem to believe, a matter of straightforward cause and
> effect."

---

## 3. Everyday American / Regional Neutral

**Who uses it:** most contemporary American characters in daily situations.

**Characteristics:**
- Natural contractions and ellipsis
- Colloquial connectives: "so", "anyway", "I mean"
- Some profanity available; register-dependent
- Sentence length: short-medium (6–12 words)
- Fragmentation: medium — sentences trail off, get interrupted, don't always finish

**Example:**
> "So anyway, she left. Just like that. I mean, what are you going to do."

---

## 4. Street / Working-Class / Vernacular

**Who uses it:** working-class characters, certain urban settings, characters under
social or economic pressure.

**Characteristics:**
- Dropped verb endings ("gonna", "wanna", "ain't")
- Double negatives as emphasis
- Territorial or community-specific idioms
- High interruption frequency
- Profanity: functional, not decorative
- Sentence length: short (4–8 words)
- Fragmentation: very high

**Example:**
> "Man, nobody told me nothing. I just showed up. That ain't on me."

---

## 5. Youth / Gen-Z / Digital

**Who uses it:** younger characters (under 30), digital-native contexts.

**Characteristics:**
- Irony as default register; sincerity requires effort
- Self-referential interjections ("honestly", "literally", "I can't")
- Mixed formality for comic or sad effect
- English borrowed vocabulary for emphasis
- Sentence length: variable — short in text, longer in monologue
- Fragmentation: high

**Example:**
> "I literally cannot with this. Like, honestly. Whatever."

---

## 6. Southern / Regional American

**Who uses it:** characters from American South; can also apply to other specific regions.

**Characteristics:**
- Region-specific syntax ("fixin' to", "might could", "y'all")
- Courtliness and indirection in formal situations
- Capable of violence in calm register — the lack of affect is the threat
- Narrative storytelling tendency (characters explain through anecdote)
- Sentence length: medium to long
- Fragmentation: low in formal register, higher under stress

**Example:**
> "Now, I've known your daddy a long time, and I am telling you, as a courtesy,
> that you do not want to go down that road."

---

## 7. Academic / Literary-Formal

**Who uses it:** scholars, certain antagonists, narrators of historical fiction.

**Characteristics:**
- Embedded relative clauses
- Passive constructions for distancing
- Vocabulary from specific field (philosophy, law, military history)
- No slang; no contractions in formal address
- Sentence length: long (18–35 words)
- Fragmentation: very low

**Example:**
> "What was understood by those present — and this point cannot be overstated —
> was that the decision had already been made before the meeting began."

---

## 8. Immigrant / Second-Language English

**Who uses it:** characters for whom English is not the first language.

**Characteristics:**
- Different idiom structures (calques from first language)
- Article usage varies from native patterns
- Formality tends to be higher than native speakers in equivalent situations
- Specific errors consistent with first language (not random errors)
- Sentence length: medium (8–14 words)
- Fragmentation: low (incomplete sentences feel wrong, even under stress)

**Example:**
> "The problem is not what you think it is. I have lived here many years.
> I know how these things work."

---

## Narrator Register Note

The narrator has its own register, separate from all characters.

Common narrator registers in literary fiction:
- `intimate-close`: near the protagonist's consciousness; free indirect discourse; "she"
  used as if "I" but with slight distance
- `detached-observer`: camera-eye; no interiority; physical detail only; present-tense common
- `literary-measured`: controlled prose; occasional earned metaphor; past tense; slight distance
- `ironic-dry`: gap between what is described and how it is described; flat affect; deadpan
- `omniscient-god`: full access; can zoom in and out; rare in literary fiction but legitimate
- `lyrical-lyric`: voice that earns its music through specificity; not decorative

Define the narrator's register in `.story-bible/tone-profile.json` under `narration_distance`.
