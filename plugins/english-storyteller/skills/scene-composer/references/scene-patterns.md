# Scene Patterns

Working examples of scene entry, body, and closure techniques.

---

## Scene Entry Patterns

### Verb-First Entry (action in progress)
> "The gate was open when it shouldn't have been."

### Aftermath Entry (scene enters after the event)
> "He was already across the bridge when he heard the truck."

### Dialogue Entry (mid-conversation)
> "'How long has it been like this?' She didn't wait for an answer."

### Sensation Entry (body before context)
> "The smoke smell reached him first. Then the sound."

### Object Entry (charged object before character)
> "The gun was on the table. Neither of them looked at it."

---

## Scene Body: Tension Building

Short sentences increase pressure. Each sentence lands before the next. No subordination.

```
The door was open. He hadn't opened it. He stepped back. Wrong room. Wrong floor.
Or right floor, wrong door. He stood in the hall.
```

---

## Scene Body: Interiority (Memory Intrusion)

Long sentences hold memory; short sentences break back to present.

```
He'd been here before — not this room but this particular quality of afternoon light
through aluminum blinds, which meant somewhere in the desert Southwest, which meant
a different decade, a different life he'd thought was over.

The phone rang.
```

---

## Scene Body: Dialogue with Physical Beats

Beats replace adverbs and emotional tags. Movement is meaning.

```
"We should go."
She hadn't moved. He had his hand on the door.
"I know."
"So."
She looked at the window. Not through it. At it.
"We should go," he said again.
```

---

## Scene Body: Action Peak

The shortest sentences appear at the moment of highest tension. Fragments allowed.

```
He ran. Three steps. Four. The noise behind him — closer now, or different, he couldn't
tell which. He turned the corner. Stopped. Dead end. A wall and no door and no way through.

He turned around.

They were there.
```

## Scene Closure Patterns

### Open Image (physical without resolution)
> "She watched him go. The light was changing. That particular light before a storm."

### Interrupted Action (scene ends before completion)
> "He reached for the phone. The door opened."

### Recontextualized Detail (early image returns with new meaning)
> *(Scene opened with: "The coffee was still burning when she swallowed it.")*
> *(Scene closes with:)* "She poured the coffee out. Both cups."

### Silence as Close
> "He said her name.
> She didn't answer.
> Outside, the street went on."

### The Body as Close
> "He sat down on the floor. Not thinking. Just not standing anymore."

---

## The Deletion Test in Practice

Before finalizing any scene, run the deletion test on every sentence:

1. Remove the sentence.
2. Read the surrounding context.
3. Has any *fact* been lost? (Not feeling — fact.)
4. If yes, keep. If no, cut.

Common sentences that fail the test:
- "It had been a long day." (Shown by the scene.)
- "She wasn't sure what to feel." (The reader is constructing the feeling already.)
- "Things between them were complicated." (The scene just showed this.)
- "He didn't know what to say." (His silence has been narrated — this is redundant.)

---

## The No-Explanation Rule

After any line of dialogue or action, check: has the narrator added an interpretation?

**With explanation (cut):**
> "'I'm fine,' she said. This was, of course, not true."

**Without explanation (keep):**
> "'I'm fine,' she said."

The reader knows. Trust them.
