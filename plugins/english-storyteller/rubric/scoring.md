# English Storyteller — Scoring Rubric

Anchor passages and descriptors for each dimension at levels 2, 5, 8, and 9+.

Use these anchors to calibrate scores. Match the prose against the closest anchor. Interpolate between bands.

---

## Dimension 1: Rhythm

Does sentence length vary deliberately? Do short sentences land at peaks? Do long sentences carry accumulation?

**Score 2 — Metronome:**
> "He picked up his weapon. He walked to the door. He opened it slowly. He looked outside."

Four sentences, identical length, identical structure. Uniform beat. No tension, no release.

**Score 5 — Some variation, pattern-predictable:**
> "He picked up his weapon and moved to the door, checking the hallway through the crack before committing to any movement. Short step. He was out."

Better — one long sentence, two short. But the pattern is predictable: long/short/short. The variation is not motivated by content.

**Score 8 — Real variation, mostly motivated:**
> "He moved. The door opened into nothing useful — a hallway, a window at the far end, somebody's coat still hanging on the rack. He'd expected — he didn't know what he'd expected. He moved."

Short / medium-long (with internal rhythm change mid-clause) / medium / short. The movement sentences are short; the inventory sentence accumulates and trails. Mostly motivated. The repeated "He moved" works but is slightly mechanical.

**Score 9+ — Rhythm as meaning:**
> "He moved. The hallway: a coat on a rack, a window showing sky, nothing threatening, nothing that explained the sound. He stood there for what felt like a long time — it was probably eight seconds — trying to decide whether the fact that he was still alive was evidence of anything. It was not. He moved again."

Short / long accumulation (inventory + cognitive process) / very short declarative / short. Every length change is motivated. The cognitive sentence is long because thought is slow; the declarative that cancels it ("It was not") is short because the answer is simple. Rhythm serves meaning.

## Dimension 2: Character Voice

Is the narrator's voice distinct and irreplaceable?

**Score 2 — Generic literary narrator:**
> "The scene was overwhelming. He had never experienced anything like this before. He tried to remain calm, but it was difficult."

Could belong to any narrator in any literary novel. No specificity, no personality, no signature vocabulary.

**Score 5 — Present but generic in stretches:**
> "The road was bad. Bodies at the edges, where the verge met the asphalt. He had been trained for this, or had been told he had been trained for this. Not the same thing."

Better. "Told he had been trained" is a good distinction. But "the road was bad" is generic, and "he had been trained for this" is a common military-narrator thought.

**Score 8 — Distinct, with one or two generic passages:**
> "The road was lined with cars that had tried to do something sensible and failed at it. You could see the logic in each wreck — get off the road, get behind this thing, get out and run — and you could see where the logic stopped working."

Voice is present. "Tried to do something sensible and failed at it" is specific. "The logic stopped working" is good. One more signature move would push this to 9.

**Score 9+ — Irreducible voice:**
> "The cars had the look of decisions. Each one stopped at the point where whoever was driving it had run out of options, or nerve, or luck — and from here they were all the same: wrong. The one with the door hanging open. The one that had made it into the ditch. The one still in the center lane as if waiting for a green light."

"Had the look of decisions" is a formulation no generic narrator produces. The three-element list at the end (specific, specific, absurdist) is signature. "Wrong" as a one-word closing declarative for an inventory — this is voice.

## Dimension 3: Continuity

Is the reader always correctly located in time, space, and emotional state?

**Score 2 — Consistently disoriented:**
> "Then they were in the city. Earlier he had seen the car. The night before, the plan had been different. He remembered the road."

No stable temporal anchor. Events float. The reader cannot reconstruct sequence.

**Score 5 — Mostly coherent, one disorienting jump:**
> "They drove south on the highway. When they arrived at the checkpoint the officer waved them through. He had been expecting hostility, not this — a wave, a nod, a thank-you."

Good emotional continuity. But the transition from driving to checkpoint arrival is abrupt — one missing sentence.

**Score 8 — Coherent throughout, minor smoothing needed:**
> "The highway south was moving. Military vehicles in both lanes, civilian cars trying to move between them, everyone going the same direction. At the checkpoint the officer just waved. He'd expected — had everyone expected? — something harder."

Well-grounded. The shift from highway to checkpoint is clear. The hedging ("had everyone expected?") is a small blip in the continuous voice.

**Score 9+ — Seamless:**
> "Highway 6 south: a river of metal. Civilian cars moving between military vehicles with the logic of water — finding gaps, stopping where blocked. At the checkpoint a single officer, waving. Nothing. He had prepared for something else at checkpoints — had held his documentation, rehearsed the reason they were there — but nobody was interested in reasons. South. Keep moving south."

Time, space, emotional state all fully inhabited. The reader knows the road, the checkpoint, the narrat's prior expectation, and the new emotional fact (relief/disorientation at no resistance). The prose moves through it seamlessly.

## Dimension 4: Prose Density

Is every word earning its place?

**Score 2 — Significant filler:**
> "It was a very difficult situation that he found himself in. He tried his best to stay calm and collected, but the truth was that he was extremely frightened by everything that was happening around him."

"Very difficult," "tried his best," "calm and collected," "the truth was," "extremely frightened," "everything that was happening" — every phrase is filler.

**Score 5 — Some compression, some filler:**
> "The situation was hard. He was frightened, he admitted that. He kept moving."

Better — "hard" instead of "very difficult." But "he admitted that" is a throat-clear. Delete.

**Score 8 — Mostly compressed:**
> "He was frightened. He kept moving."

Two sentences, no waste. Clean. Can this be compressed further? Possibly: "Frightened. Kept moving." Depends on POV conventions.

**Score 9+ — Maximum compression, every word load-bearing:**
> "Frightened was the wrong word for it. He moved."

"Frightened was the wrong word for it" is denser than "he was frightened" — it contains the emotion, its inaccuracy, and the narrator's awareness of the inaccuracy in seven words. "He moved" then enacts what thought cannot.

## Dimension 5: POV Consistency

Does the camera stay fixed?

**Score 2 — Systematic violations:**
> "He didn't know what the other soldiers were thinking. They were scared too, Rabi especially — Rabi was thinking about his wife."

First sentence announces the POV limit. Second sentence violates it by entering Rabi's thoughts.

**Score 5 — Mostly consistent, one slip:**
> "He couldn't see Rabi's face from this angle. But Rabi seemed fine. He was probably fine."

"He couldn't see" — correct. "Seemed fine" — borderline, the narrator can infer appearance. "He was probably fine" — narrator's inference, valid. Mostly clean.

**Score 8 — Consistent with minor hedge:**
> "Rabi's voice sounded the same as always. Either he was holding it together or he was very good at sounding like it."

The narrator observes voice (external access, valid) and then explicitly marks the epistemic limit ("either... or"). Good technique. Minor: the hedge is slightly mechanical.

**Score 9+ — Perfect lock, POV limit made meaningful:**
> "Rabi said nothing, which meant either everything was fine or nothing was fine and Rabi had decided it wasn't worth talking about. He couldn't tell. He'd never been able to tell with Rabi."

The POV limit ("couldn't tell") is not just a constraint — it is characterization. Rabi is unreadable. The narrator's inability to access Rabi's interiority is information about their relationship.

## Dimension 6: Emotional Register

Does the tone stay locked within each scene?

**Score 2 — Constant oscillation:**
> "The street was a scene of devastation. He felt a deep sadness at what humanity had wrought. But then he squared his shoulders — there was work to do. The sun was setting beautifully on the ruins."

Grief → determination → lyrical beauty — three register shifts in four sentences.

**Score 5 — One tonal departure:**
> "The bodies were past counting. He stopped counting. There was nothing to do with the number anyway."

Good flat affect maintained for two sentences. "Nothing to do with the number anyway" is a slight tonal drift — a little too philosophical for the scene's register.

**Score 8 — Consistent, one barely-noticeable departure:**
> "He counted. He stopped. There were too many. He didn't need a number."

Flat and locked. "He didn't need a number" is almost too clean — one phrase away from aphorism, which is one phrase away from register drift.

**Score 9+ — Register locked, tone serves content:**
> "He tried to count. The count stopped making sense somewhere after forty. He wrote '40+' on a piece of paper and put it in his pocket."

The action of writing it down — bureaucratic, flat, almost absurd — performs the emotional content without naming it. The register is flat-affect military throughout. The "40+" on paper is the only lyrical element, and it's anti-lyrical by nature.

## Dimension 7: Show-Don't-Tell

Is emotion implied through behavior and image rather than stated?

**Score 2 — Systematic tell:**
> "He felt devastated by what he saw. He was horrified. The sorrow was overwhelming and he knew that this moment would stay with him forever."

Every sentence names an emotion. No image. No behavior. The prose is an emotional label list.

**Score 5 — Mix of show and tell:**
> "He saw the stroller and stopped walking. Red. Holes in the fabric at the back, where the child would have sat. He was disturbed by this, he couldn't look at it, he kept walking."

First three sentences show (stopping, the observation, the specific detail). Last sentence tells (disturbed, couldn't look) and then shows (kept walking). Cut the tell; the keeping-walking is stronger alone.

**Score 8 — Mostly shown, one residual tell:**
> "He saw the stroller. Red. Four holes in the back panel where the child would have sat, where a child had sat. He kept walking."

Clean. The stroller observation, the holes, the tense shift (would/had — the certainty creeping in), the keeping walking. No emotional label. One residual: "where a child had sat" verges on editorializing. Cut to "where the child had been," or just let the description stop at the holes.

**Score 9+ — Fully shown, subtext fully operative:**
> "The stroller: red, capsized, the back panel punched through in three or four places at the height where a child sits. He looked at it for a moment. Then he was past it."

The observations are clinical. "At the height where a child sits" is an anatomical fact, not an emotional one — but the reader cannot hear it without the emotion. "He was past it" — past the stroller, past the emotion he did not name, past this part of the road. All three simultaneously.

## Dimension 8: Reference Fidelity

Does the prose apply the craft moves from the primary benchmark?

**Score 2 — No benchmark influence:**
> "The battle was intense. He fought bravely. When it was over, he felt relieved."

Could be written without knowledge of any specific author. Generic military narrative. No craft move from any benchmark visible.

**Score 5 — Benchmark consulted, techniques partially applied:**
> "He moved through the position. The equipment was scattered — radios, ammunition, someone's water bottle still full. He'd been in worse positions."

Klay-adjacent in flatness. But the catalog is not earning its weight the way Klay's does. O'Brien's list technique would make the inventory work harder. The final sentence is Wolff-adjacent understatement but not precise enough.

**Score 8 — 3-4 craft moves clearly applied:**
> "He moved through what remained of the position. A radio still on. Two M4s against the wall, which meant their owners were not coming back for them. A water bottle, full. He wrote a note — 'Pos. 4: equipment, weapons, no personnel' — and put it in his shirt pocket."

O'Brien specificity (exact weapon type). Klay's flat institutional note ("no personnel" for "everyone is dead"). Junger's quantification habit. Three benchmarks in four sentences.

**Score 9+ — Primary benchmark fingerprint unmistakable, all craft moves applied:**
> "The position held: two M4s, a radio still cycling through channels, three full water bottles, six magazines, a chest rig, and one boot. Just the one. These were the things that remained when the men carrying them were gone, and they remained with the patience of objects, which is a perfect patience, waiting for whoever came next. He wrote '14-A: equipment secure, no personnel' and folded the note into his left breast pocket. He would forget which pocket."

O'Brien's catalog-as-weight (the objects that outlast the men). Loyd's lyricism ("the patience of objects... a perfect patience"). Klay's institutional notation. Junger's specific detail (one boot). Wolff's closing self-deprecating irony (he will forget which pocket). All five corpus authors present in one extended passage at 9+ level.

## Override Notes

**Do NOT penalize register consistency for an intentionally flat narrator.** Military realism often uses flat affect as the dominant register throughout. Score 10 for Dimension 6 if flat affect is consistent and deliberate — consistency at any register level is the standard, not warmth.

**Do NOT penalize show-don't-tell for a single naming of emotion if it is earned.** After a full scene of behavioral/imagistic showing, one short declarative that names the emotion ("He was frightened") can be a 9+ move if it lands as understatement against a background of showing. Score the ratio, not the presence of a single instance.

**Reference Fidelity requires knowing the benchmark.** If 02-reference-finder produced no primary benchmark (offline, or search failed), score Dimension 8 against the corpus in `${CLAUDE_PLUGIN_ROOT}/corpus/`. Default primary benchmark: Phil Klay (*Redeployment*) for contemporary military realism; Tim O'Brien (*The Things They Carried*) for Vietnam/historical.
