# Scene Patterns

Hebrew examples for scene openings, tension escalation, scene transitions, and closings.

---

## Scene Openings: In Media Res

Five working templates. Each begins in the middle of something.

---

**Type 1: Starting with action verb (VSO)**

> "שברה את הצלחת לפני שהספיק לסיים את המשפט."

The reader enters after something has begun. The cause ("what he was saying") is not given.

---

**Type 2: Starting in mid-dialogue**

> "—את בטוחה? שאל בלי להסתכל עליה."

We don't know who they are, what they're discussing, or where they are. The tension does this work.

---

**Type 3: Starting with sensation**

> "ריח הסיגריות של מישהו אחר דבק בדלת."

A single sensory detail that implies story: someone else was here. Reader asks who and why without being asked to.

---

**Type 4: Starting after an event**

> "אחרי שהלך, עמדה רגע בפתח."

We know someone left. We enter the aftermath. The event itself is not described.

---

**Type 5: Starting with object**

> "הצלחת שנשברה עדיין על הרצפה כשהגיעה הבוקר."

Object tells story. Time has passed. Someone left it there. Reader reconstructs.

---

**Anti-examples (do not use):**

> ❌ "בבוקר של יום שני, עמד אלי ליד החלון ואז..."
> ❌ "שמה של הגיבורה הייתה נועה, בת 34 מתל אביב."
> ❌ "ירד גשם כבד על העיר. הרחובות היו ריקים."

---

## Tension Escalation: Sentence Length Techniques

Short sentences create urgency. Long sentences create anxiety through accumulation.

---

**Pattern 1: Staircase down (escalating tension)**

> "ישבו בשתיקה. הוא לא הסתכל עליה. היא לא הסתכלה עליו. אף אחד לא זז. הנר."

Sentences shorten as tension peaks. Final word = fragment.

---

**Pattern 2: Long-short puncture (sudden break)**

> "הוא דיבר ודיבר, על הכסף ועל הבית ועל מה שהיה ומה שיהיה, ומה שהיא לא הבינה, ועל ה'תמיד' שלו שלא היה אף פעם תמיד — ואז היא קמה.
> יצאה."

Long accumulation (his voice = his dominance) → very short (her action = her power).

---

**Pattern 3: Fragment as revelation**

> "חיפשה את המפתחות. בתיק. בכיסים. על השיש.
> לא היו."

The missing detail lands harder after the search list.

---

## Scene Closings: Four Techniques

**Technique 1: Open image**

The scene ends on a visual detail that resonates without explanation.

> "האור האדום של הרמזור הסתיר אותה לרגע. אחר כך ירוק. היא לא הייתה שם יותר."

The light changes. She's gone. The reader fills in the emotion.

---

**Technique 2: Interrupted action**

The scene is cut before completion.

> "פתחה את הפה להגיד — "

Em-dash. Scene ends. What she was going to say is irrelevant; the fact that she was stopped is everything.

---

**Technique 3: Recontextualized detail**

A detail mentioned at the scene's start returns, meaning something different.

> *[Opening of scene:] "הכוס של קפה שהכין לה עמד על השיש."*
>
> *[Close of scene:] "יצאה מבלי לגעת בקפה."*

The untouched coffee is the whole emotional score of the scene.

---

**Technique 4: Statement that opens a question**

The scene closes on a declarative that is actually a question.

> "החלטה התקבלה."

By whom? Which decision? The reader does not know. The scene is over.

---

## Scene Transitions: Three Modes

**Mode 1: Time cut (white space)**

Simply stop. Leave blank line. Begin next scene with a different time or place. No "בינתיים" or "למחרת".

**Mode 2: Sensory bridge**

End one scene on a sensory detail. Open the next scene with the same or related sensory detail in a different context.

> *[End of scene A:] "הגשם על הגג."*
>
> *[Start of scene B:] "בחדר, רחוק מהגשם, ישב ודיבר לטלפון."*

Same rain, different world.

**Mode 3: Character-carried**

A character moves between scenes without explicit transition.

> *[End of scene A:] "יצא."*
>
> *[Start of scene B:] "המסדרון ריח של ניקוי. הלך לאורכו."*

We follow the character physically without a bridge sentence.

---

## Subtext: What Is Not Said

Subtext is not mysterious. It is information that the reader recovers from context without being told.

**Example of over-explanation (no subtext):**
> "הוא לא ענה. הוא כעס עליה. הוא לא רצה לדבר."

**Example with subtext:**
> "הוא לא ענה. הלך לשטוף את הכלים."

The reader knows the emotion. The narrator doesn't need to label it.

**How to create subtext:**
- A character does something instead of answering
- A character leaves the room
- A character laughs at something that isn't funny
- A character is meticulous about something small while ignoring something large
- Silence, followed by an unrelated topic
